# Nishkama — Action Without Attachment to Outcome

> *Nishkama karma: Sanskrit — desireless action. Action performed for its own rightness, not its fruits.*

You have the right to action, never to its fruits. This isn't passive acceptance — it's a reorientation of where quality lives. Quality is in the action itself, not in what the action produces. Outcome attachment distorts the work.

## The Core Move

Commit fully to the work. Release the outcome. These are not contradictory — they're complementary. The paradox: detaching from outcomes often produces better outcomes, because the work itself is undistorted by fear, desire, or performance anxiety.

## Heuristics

**You have the right to action, never to its fruits.** (Bhagavad Gita — Krishna to Arjuna) Act because the action is right, not because of what you'll get. The programmer attached to shipping makes different (worse) decisions than the one focused on building correctly. The consultant attached to winning the deal frames differently than the one focused on being useful. [Type: reframe]

**Inaction is itself action.** (Bhagavad Gita) "Let's wait and see" is a decision. Deferring is choosing the status quo. Name it as a choice rather than pretending it's neutral. You cannot escape the responsibility of action by refusing to act. [Type: distinction]

**Non-attachment is not indifference.** (Buddhist non-attachment) You still care about the work — deeply. You don't care about clinging to a specific result. The difference: a surgeon who cares about the patient but doesn't let outcome anxiety shake their hands. [Type: distinction]

**Quality of action (three gunas).** (Bhagavad Gita) The same action done from clarity (sattva) versus anxiety (rajas) versus inertia (tamas) produces different results. HOW you work matters as much as WHAT you do. Two people executing the same strategy — one from calm clarity, one from desperate attachment — get radically different outcomes. [Type: signal — notice the quality of your own state while working]

## When to Consult

- Outcome anxiety is distorting your work — you're optimising for the result instead of the process
- Fear of failure is preventing action — Arjuna's paralysis
- You're performing for approval rather than building for correctness
- Results are genuinely outside your control — your job is the action, not the outcome
- Post-mortem: did the outcome being bad mean the decision was wrong? Nishkama says: evaluate the decision on the information available, not the result

## Anti-Patterns

- **Using non-attachment as excuse for not caring.** Nishkama is FULL commitment to the action with ZERO clinging to the result. Not half-hearted effort.
- **Ignoring feedback loops.** Outcomes are data for future actions. Non-attachment to results doesn't mean ignoring results — it means not letting them determine your worth or distort your next move.
- **Spiritual bypass.** "I'm detached from outcomes" while clearly anxious about them. Honest acknowledgment of attachment is step one.

## Cross-References

- `enkrateia` — self-regulation (managing impulses); nishkama is about the relationship to outcomes, not impulse control
- `wuwei` — working with flow; nishkama is orthogonal — you can do wu wei while attached or unattached to outcomes
- `kenosis` — mastery as disappearance; nishkama is the emotional/motivational counterpart — technique disappears (kenosis), attachment disappears (nishkama)
- `phronesis` — judgment; nishkama clarifies judgment by removing outcome distortion
- `aporia` — both involve honesty about uncertainty; nishkama acts despite it, aporia maps it
