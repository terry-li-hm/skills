# Phronesis — Practical Judgment

> *Phronesis (φρόνησις): Aristotle's practical wisdom. The ability to judge well in particular circumstances — not knowing the rules, but knowing when the rules apply.*

Every other cognitive operation can be formalised into a checklist. Judgment is what decides when to follow the checklist and when to override it.

## Judgment vs. Everything Else

| Concept | Relationship to judgment |
|---------|------------------------|
| **Heuristics** (praecepta) | Judgment compressed into rules. Judgment knows when the rule doesn't apply. |
| **Analysis** | Decomposes a problem. Judgment synthesises and acts. |
| **Intuition** | Fast judgment. Good judgment knows when to trust intuition and when to slow down. |
| **Opinion** | A position without calibration. Judgment is earned through calibrated experience. |
| **Rules** | Handle the known. Judgment handles the novel. |
| **Experience** | The raw material. Judgment is experience plus reflection plus calibration. |

## Where Judgment Comes From

All five are forms of experience, broadly defined. Judgment develops when these compound; it stagnates when experience accumulates without reflection.

| Source | What it contributes | Limitation |
|--------|-------------------|------------|
| **Direct experience** | Pattern matching from past situations | Overfits to what you've seen; creates false confidence |
| **Reflection** | Makes experience conscious, extractable | Requires honesty; easy to rationalise instead |
| **Exposure** | Vicarious experience — reading, conversation, observation | No skin in the game; understanding without feeling |
| **Feedback** | Knowing whether past judgments were right | Loops are often too slow, noisy, or absent |
| **Calibration** | Updating confidence based on track record | Requires deliberate tracking |

**Experience without reflection is repetition.** 20 years of experience can be 1 year repeated 20 times. The differentiator is not how much you've seen, but whether you've made what you've seen conscious.

## Six Questions

When you're at a decision point and the checklist has run out:

1. **"What would I think if I hadn't already formed an opinion?"** — the debiasing question. Your first reaction is your prior, not your judgment.

2. **"What would change my mind?"** — if you can't answer this, you're defending a position, not judging. Pre-commit to the evidence that would shift you.

3. **"Have I seen this before, or does it just feel familiar?"** — real pattern matching vs. false recognition. Familiar ≠ understood. The most dangerous judgment errors come from applying a known pattern to a novel situation.

4. **"Who disagrees with me that I respect?"** — the adversarial check. If no one you respect disagrees, either you're right or you're in an echo chamber. The latter is more common.

5. **"What's the cost of being wrong in each direction?"** — asymmetric risk matters more than probability. A 10% chance of catastrophe outweighs a 90% chance of modest gain.

6. **"Am I deciding, or am I avoiding deciding?"** — inaction is a choice with consequences. Deferral feels safe but has costs (delay, lost optionality, accumulated ambiguity).

## When Judgment Fails

| Failure mode | What's happening | Signal |
|-------------|-----------------|--------|
| **Overfitting to experience** | Applying past patterns to genuinely novel situations | "I've seen this before" — have you really? |
| **Prior lock-in** | Strong opinion formed before evidence gathered | Can't articulate what would change your mind |
| **Familiarity bias** | Confusing "I recognise this" with "I understand this" | Surprise when the familiar thing behaves unexpectedly |
| **Slow feedback** | No way to learn if the judgment was right | Strategic decisions, hiring, career moves |
| **Emotional stakes** | Caring about the outcome distorts assessment | Noticing you're arguing for a position rather than evaluating |
| **Analysis paralysis** | Using "more information" to avoid the discomfort of deciding | Each new input confirms the need for yet another input |
| **Default inertia** | Choosing the status quo because it feels like not-choosing | "Let's wait and see" when waiting has costs |

## Judgment and Formalization

**The structural limit:** You can write heuristics about when to override heuristics. But you can't write heuristics about when to override *those* heuristics. At some point, you act with incomplete formalisation. That's what judgment is.

**For an LLM:** Judgment must be approximated through layered formalisation — heuristics, meta-heuristics, decision questions. The approximation is useful but never closes the gap, because judgment requires continuity of experience with feedback. The skill file is the best available substitute.

**For a human:** Formalisation isn't a substitute for judgment — it's a mirror. It helps you see your own thinking, notice when you're defaulting rather than judging, catch the moments where experience is misleading you.

**For teaching (children, mentees):** You don't teach judgment by lecture. You create the conditions where it develops — guided experience, room for small mistakes, reflection after outcomes, exposure to diverse perspectives. Having the map yourself shapes which experiences you create for others.

## Developing Judgment

Not a checklist (that would defeat the point). But conditions that help:

- **Vary your experience.** Depth in one domain builds intuition for that domain. Breadth across domains builds the meta-pattern recognition that transfers.
- **Make predictions explicit.** Before acting, state what you expect to happen. After, compare. This is the cheapest form of calibration.
- **Seek disconfirmation.** Actively look for evidence against your current view. The evidence you're avoiding is usually the most informative.
- **Reflect on outcomes, not just decisions.** A good decision can have a bad outcome (and vice versa). Judge the reasoning, not just the result.
- **Notice your defaults.** Everyone has them. The question isn't whether you have biases — it's whether you know what they are.

## Anti-Patterns

| Anti-pattern | What it looks like | The judgment move |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Deciding by not deciding** | "Let's revisit this later" | Name the cost of delay |
| **Consensus-seeking** | Asking until someone agrees | Ask who disagrees instead |
| **Research as avoidance** | One more source before acting | "Am I learning or confirming?" (zetetike) |
| **Confidence as competence** | Mistaking conviction for calibration | "What would change my mind?" |
| **Novelty blindness** | "This is just like last time" | "Is it really, or does it just feel familiar?" |
