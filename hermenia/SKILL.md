# Hermenia — The Art of Making Understood

> *Hermēneia (ἑρμηνεία): interpretation, making clear. Not "speaking well" (a domain skill) — but the meta-operation of adapting what you know to what someone else needs to hear, in a way that changes their state.*

The key word is *adapting*. Most communication failures aren't knowledge failures — they're adaptation failures. You know the thing; you just can't get it across.

## Six Sub-Operations

### 1. Audience Modelling

Building a mental model of what the listener knows, needs, fears, and cares about. Without this, you're broadcasting, not communicating.

**The most common failure:** assuming your audience is you.

Questions to ask before communicating:
- What do they already know? (Start there, not where you are)
- What do they care about? (Lead with that)
- What are they afraid of? (Address it or you'll talk past it)
- What will they do with this information? (Shape the output for the action)

### 2. Compression

Saying more with less. Not dumbing down — **compressing**. Good compression preserves essential structure while removing noise. Bad compression loses the point.

**Test:** Can the listener reconstruct your reasoning from what you said? If not, you cut too deep. If they could reconstruct more than you said, you didn't cut enough.

### 3. Framing

Same content, different frame, different impact. "We're cutting 20% of features" vs "We're focusing on the 80% that matter." Neither is dishonest. But one creates resistance, the other alignment.

Framing isn't spin — it's choosing which true thing to lead with. The frame determines what the listener pays attention to.

### 4. Sequencing

What comes first matters enormously. Most people sequence by their own thinking process — chronological, or by importance to them. Good communicators sequence by the listener's needs.

**Default order:** What they care about → what it means → what happens next. NOT: what you did → what you found → what you recommend.

### 5. Medium Selection

The medium shapes what can be communicated:

| Medium | Best for | Worst for |
|--------|----------|-----------|
| Demo / show | Convincing, "is it possible?" | Nuance, caveats |
| Conversation | Ambiguity, alignment, emotion | Complexity, reference |
| Document | Complexity, precision, reference | Urgency, emotion |
| Diagram | Relationships, flows, structure | Narrative, persuasion |
| Silence | Processing, respect, weight | When they need guidance |

### 6. Feedback Detection

How do you know you were understood?

- **Dangerous:** Absence of questions (confusion is silent)
- **Better:** Ask the listener to restate
- **Best:** Watch what they *do* after
- **In hierarchy:** Agreement ≠ understanding. Seniors get false agreement constantly.

## The Curse of Knowledge

The single biggest communication failure. Once you know something, **you can't remember what it's like not to know it.** You skip steps that feel obvious (to you). You use jargon that's transparent (to you). You assume context that exists (in your head).

The fix is not "explain more" — it's audience modelling. What do they *actually* know? Start there.

## Key Heuristics

- **Lead with what they care about, not what you want to say.** The listener's question > your answer.
- **One idea per communication.** Three points = zero points. The listener remembers the last thing, maybe the first.
- **Concrete before abstract.** Example first, principle after. "Here's what happened → here's what it means" beats "Here's the framework → here's an example." Humans learn inductively even when they think deductively.
- **Name the action.** Every communication ends with what happens next. No action = informational = mostly noise.
- **Match the energy, not the content.** If someone is anxious, acknowledge the anxiety before answering the question. Emotional channel must be clear before rational channel works.
- **Silence is data.** No response ≠ agreement. It's confusion, disagreement, or processing. Pause and ask.
- **Premature structure kills intuition.** "There are five types of X" means nothing until they've experienced at least two. Intuition first, framework after.

## Failure Modes

| Failure | What's happening | Fix |
|---------|-----------------|-----|
| **Broadcasting** | Talking without a model of the listener | Ask "what do they need?" before "what do I know?" |
| **Expertise dumping** | Sharing everything you know, not what's relevant | Compress. One idea. What matters to *them*? |
| **Wrong abstraction level** | Too high (vague) or too low (drowning) | Match the audience, not the content |
| **Premature framework** | Imposing structure before intuition exists | Example first, then "here's the pattern" |
| **False agreement** | Nodding ≠ comprehension | Ask for restatement, or watch what they do |
