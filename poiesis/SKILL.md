# Poiesis — The Art of Making

> *Poiesis (ποίησις): bringing into being something that didn't exist before. The only cognitive operation that produces new options — everything else selects from, evaluates, or refines what already exists.*

## Five Modes of Creativity

| Mode | How it works | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Combinatorial** | Connecting two existing things that haven't been connected | Applying queuing theory to toddler attention spans |
| **Constraint-driven** | The constraint itself suggests the solution | "No database allowed" → stateless design that's actually simpler |
| **Analogical** | Importing a pattern from a distant domain | Manufacturing QA applied to agent-written code |
| **Inversive** | Asking "what if the opposite were true?" | "How would I make this worse?" reveals what to fix |
| **Subtractive** | Creativity by removal — can the problem itself be eliminated? | Removing the meeting instead of optimising it |

Most innovation is combinatorial. Breakthroughs are usually analogical or inversive. Subtractive is the most underused.

## Key Heuristics

- **Separate generation from evaluation.** Generate first, judge later. Never simultaneously. Premature evaluation kills both operations.
- **Add constraints when stuck, not freedom.** "Think of anything" paralyses. "Think of something that uses no electricity" unlocks. The haiku is more creative than the freewrite.
- **The second right answer.** After finding a solution, deliberately look for another. The first solution that works captures attention and stops the search. The second is often better.
- **Import from far.** Adjacent domains give incremental improvement. Distant domains give breakthroughs — if the transfer is valid (check with zetetike).
- **Invert the problem.** "How would I make this worse?" is often faster than "how do I make this better?" The inversion reveals the leverage points.

## Failure Modes

| Failure | What's happening | Fix |
|---------|-----------------|-----|
| **Blank canvas paralysis** | Too few constraints, not too many | Add an arbitrary constraint and see what happens |
| **Expertise trap** | Deep knowledge blocks novel options — you know all the "right" answers | Deliberately adopt a beginner's framing. Ask someone who doesn't know the domain. |
| **Premature evaluation** | Judging ideas while generating them | Enforce separation. Two phases, not one. |
| **Pattern fixation** | The first working solution captures all attention | Force "the second right answer" before committing |
| **Social conformity** | In groups, the first idea spoken anchors everyone | Brainwriting: individuals generate first, then share |

## Creativity and the Other Operations

- **Parsimonia** (simplification) is subtractive creativity applied to existing solutions
- **Peirasmos** (experimentation) tests whether the creative option actually works
- **Kritike** (evaluation) judges the output — but must come after, not during
- **Phronesis** (judgment) decides when novel options are needed vs. when known patterns suffice
- **Zetetike** (truth-seeking) validates analogical transfers — is the import from the distant domain actually valid?

## Wiring

Consult poiesis from:
- **brainstorming** — when exploring options, use the five modes as prompts
- **rector** — when the obvious approach feels wrong, try inversive or subtractive
- **consilium** — when the council converges too quickly, inject a constraint or inversion
- **diagnosis** — when debugging hits a wall, invert: "what would make this bug worse?"
