# Adversaria — Adversarial Perspective

> *Adversaria: Latin — notes taken from the opposing side. Things turned to face you.*

Different from empathy (understanding feelings) and inversion (imagining failure). Adversaria is adopting the opponent's strategic viewpoint to predict their moves. You don't need to like them — you need to see what they see.

## The Core Move

Step into the other side's position. See the situation from their perspective — not to connect, but to predict. What information do they have? What incentives drive them? What would you do in their position?

## Heuristics

**Become the opponent (敵になるということ).** (Musashi) "Think of yourself as the enemy." In combat, in debugging, in strategy — adopt the adversary's view. What does the bug think it's doing? What does the competitor see? What does the regulator care about? [Type: if-then trigger — before predicting someone's move, inhabit their position first]

**Know them, know yourself (知彼知己).** (Sun Tzu) Not just self-awareness — the pairing matters. It's not enough to know your strengths; you must know them relative to the specific opponent and situation. Absolute self-knowledge without relative positioning is half the picture. [Type: distinction — absolute vs. relative knowledge]

**Empty and full (虚实 xū shí).** (Sun Tzu) Probe for where the opponent is strong (full) and weak (empty). But also: appear empty where you're full, full where you're empty. Perception management is itself a strategic tool. [Type: rule]

**Orthodox and unorthodox (奇正 qí zhèng).** (Sun Tzu) The expected approach pins them in place; the unexpected breaks through. Predictability is a vulnerability. But pure unpredictability is chaos. The art is alternating — establish a pattern, then break it. [Type: spectrum]

**Inversion from the other side.** (Munger, partially) Munger's inversion asks "how would I guarantee failure?" Adversaria asks "what would I do if I were on the other side trying to win?" Overlapping but different — inversion is self-directed, adversaria is other-directed. [Type: distinction]

## When to Consult

- Debugging — what is the system "trying" to do? What inputs does the bug respond to?
- Competitive strategy — what does the competitor see, know, and optimise for?
- Negotiation prep — what does the counterparty want, fear, and know?
- Anticipating objections — what would a critic say? Not a strawman — a steelman.
- Security review — what does the attacker see? What surface is exposed?
- Evaluation design — what would a system that's gaming your metrics do?

## Anti-Patterns

- **Projection disguised as perspective-taking.** You're not imagining what you'd do — you're imagining what THEY'D do given THEIR constraints, information, and incentives. These differ.
- **Adversarial thinking when collaboration is needed.** Not every situation has an opponent. Using adversaria in a collaborative context poisons trust.
- **Paralysis by imagined opponents.** The point is prediction, not fear. Use adversaria to prepare, then act.
