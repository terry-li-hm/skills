# Invariance — Find What Doesn't Change

> *Invariance: that which remains unchanged under transformation.*

Metanoia says: change how you see the problem. Invariance says: find what STAYS when you change how you see the problem. The thing that's the same from every angle is the most fundamental thing.

## The Core Move

In a complex, shifting situation, find what's conserved. Reason from the invariant, not the flux. What doesn't change when everything else does is telling you something load-bearing about the system.

## Heuristics

**Symmetry constrains possibility.** (Physics) If a system looks the same after a transformation, that invariance limits what can happen. Every symmetry implies a conservation law. *When a problem looks the same from two perspectives, that agreement IS information — it tells you what's fundamental.* [Type: signal]

**Check the dimensions before the calculation.** (Physics — dimensional analysis) If your answer has the wrong shape, it's wrong regardless of the work. Sanity-check the form of the answer before grinding through the details. *Does this estimate make sense at the order-of-magnitude level?* [Type: rule]

**Conservation as shortcut.** (Physics) Energy is conserved. Momentum is conserved. Instead of tracking every interaction, track what's preserved. *In any complex system, find the zero-sum quantities. Budget, headcount, trust, attention — what's conserved? Reason from there.* [Type: if-then trigger — when a system is too complex to track, find its conserved quantities]

**Solve the easy version first.** (Physics — perturbation theory) Can't solve the full problem? Solve the simple version, then add small corrections. The 80% solution reveals whether you're on the right track before you invest in the remaining 20%. [Type: ordering]

## When to Consult

- System is complex and chaotic — find what's stable
- Two perspectives disagree on everything except one thing — that one thing is probably real
- Sanity-checking — does the shape of the answer make sense before you check the details?
- Looking for leverage — the invariant is often the lever, because everything else depends on it

## Anti-Patterns

- **False invariants.** Just because something hasn't changed yet doesn't mean it can't. Test whether it's truly conserved or just slow-moving.
- **Invariance tunnel vision.** Focusing only on what's conserved and ignoring the dynamic parts. The flux is where the action is — invariants are anchors for reasoning, not the whole picture.
