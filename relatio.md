# Relatio — Consulting Communication Patterns

> *Relatio: Latin "formal report, account to the senate." The structured communication that moves decisions through hierarchy.*

> Mined via fodina Tier 1. Field-validate over first Capco engagements.

Heuristics for consulting communication in financial services. Not templates — judgment calls about what to say, when, to whom, and in what shape. Complements `hermenia` (general communication theory) with consulting-specific patterns where the audience is a client, a steering committee, or a partner chain.

---

## Core Reframe

**Consulting communication is not informing — it is managing perception of progress, risk, and value.** Every update, email, and deck is simultaneously doing three jobs: reporting status, shaping expectations, and protecting the relationship. The consultant who treats communication as "just keeping people informed" will be surprised when a well-run project gets cancelled because the client felt out of control.

The inverse is also true: a struggling project with excellent communication survives longer, gets more support, and recovers more often than a struggling project with poor communication. Communication does not replace delivery — but it buys the time and trust that delivery needs.

## Key Distinctions

| Looks similar | Actually different | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Status update vs. narrative | Status = what happened. Narrative = what it means and where it's going. | Clients read status reports for the narrative, not the line items. A list of completed tasks with no "so what" feels like the project is on autopilot. |
| Transparency vs. oversharing | Transparency = sharing what the client needs to make decisions. Oversharing = dumping internal process noise that creates anxiety without enabling action. | "We explored three approaches and selected B because of X" is transparent. "We tried A, it failed, then C didn't work either, then B sort of worked" is oversharing that erodes confidence. |
| Escalation vs. complaint | Escalation = structured request for a decision or resource from someone with authority. Complaint = naming a problem without a proposed resolution or clear ask. | Complaints travel sideways and down. Escalations travel up. Never send a complaint disguised as an escalation. |
| Managing up vs. managing across | Up = your partner/engagement lead needs confidence that the project is under control. Across = peer consultants and client counterparts need coordination and shared ownership. | The same information, framed differently. Up: "We've identified a risk and here's the mitigation plan." Across: "I need your input on how to handle this risk before I escalate." |
| Delivering news vs. managing the reaction | Bad news has two components: the fact and the emotional response. Most consultants focus on softening the fact. The skill is managing the response — giving the recipient a path forward so the emotion has somewhere to go. | "The timeline has slipped by 3 weeks" lands differently than "The timeline has slipped by 3 weeks. Here's the revised plan and the two decisions we need from you to hold the new date." |
| Client silence vs. client satisfaction | Silence from a client is almost never satisfaction. It is disengagement, confusion, or building frustration that hasn't found its moment. | Treat silence as a risk signal. Reach out. "I want to make sure we're aligned on X — any concerns I should know about?" |
| Formal communication vs. the real conversation | The steering committee deck is theatre. The real decisions happen in the 1:1 before the meeting, the hallway after, or the WhatsApp message that evening. | Never let a steering committee be the first time a stakeholder hears something important. Pre-wire every significant message. |

## Client Updates

### The Update Hierarchy

Not all updates are equal. Match the communication weight to the signal importance.

| Level | Channel | Cadence | Content | When to use |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Pulse** | Chat/email, 2-3 lines | Ad hoc, 2-3x/week | "Completed X. Starting Y. No blockers." | Ongoing work, no decisions needed |
| **Status report** | Email or shared doc | Weekly | Progress, risks, decisions needed, next week's plan | Standard engagement rhythm |
| **Steering committee** | Deck + meeting | Biweekly or monthly | Strategic progress, key decisions, escalations | Governance cadence |
| **Executive summary** | 1-page memo | Milestone-triggered | What was delivered, what it means, what's next | Phase gates, major deliverables |
| **Escalation** | Direct call/email to sponsor | As needed | Problem, impact, options, recommendation | When the project needs help |

**Default with override:** Weekly status report is the baseline. Override upward (daily pulses) when: project is in crisis, client is anxious, or the first 2 weeks of an engagement. Override downward (biweekly) only when: long-running steady-state work with a trusting client.

### The Status Report Skeleton

Every status report, regardless of format, answers five questions in this order:

1. **Where are we?** (Overall health: on track / at risk / off track — one sentence)
2. **What happened this period?** (Accomplishments — concrete, not "continued working on")
3. **What's coming next period?** (Planned activities — specific enough to be falsifiable)
4. **What's at risk?** (Named risks with owner and mitigation, not vague concerns)
5. **What do we need from you?** (Decisions, access, people, approvals — explicit asks with deadlines)

**The most common failure:** Burying the ask at the bottom. If you need a decision, lead with it. "We need your sign-off on X by Friday to hold the timeline. Here's the context..."

### RAG Status (Red/Amber/Green)

Universal in consulting, universally gamed. Heuristics for honest RAG:

- **Green** means: on track, no intervention needed. Not "things are fine-ish."
- **Amber** means: at risk, mitigation is in progress but the client should be aware. Most projects should be amber most of the time — if everything is always green, you're either lying or the project is too easy.
- **Red** means: off track, requires intervention from outside the project team. Turning something red is not failure — it's asking for help before it's too late.

**The RAG ratchet:** Never downgrade from red to green in one step. Red → amber → green. Jumping straight to green after a red signals either that the red was theatrical or that you're minimising a problem that wasn't actually solved. Stakeholders notice.

**When your partner wants you to keep it green:** This happens. The partner has commercial pressure to keep the client happy. Your job is to give them the real picture privately, let them decide how to present it, but never write a status report you know is false. The line: "I'm comfortable presenting amber with this mitigation plan. I'm not comfortable presenting green when we don't have the data access we need."

## Escalation Patterns

### When to Escalate

**Rule:** Escalate when the problem exceeds your authority or resources, not when it exceeds your comfort. Discomfort is not an escalation trigger — a missing decision, a blocked resource, or a timeline breach is.

### The Escalation Email Structure

Every escalation email has exactly four components:

1. **The problem** — one sentence, factual, no editorializing. "The data migration is blocked because IT has not provisioned the staging environment, which was due on March 10."
2. **The impact** — quantified in terms the recipient cares about. Time, money, or reputation. "This delays UAT by 2 weeks and puts the June go-live at risk."
3. **Options** — at least two, with trade-offs. Never escalate with only one option (that's a demand, not an escalation) or no options (that's a complaint). "Option A: extend timeline by 2 weeks. Option B: proceed with a partial dataset and accept higher defect risk in UAT."
4. **Your recommendation** — what you'd do and why. "I recommend Option A because the cost of defects in production exceeds the cost of the delay."

**The meta-message of a good escalation:** "I've thought about this, I have a plan, I need your authority to execute it." Not: "There's a problem and I don't know what to do."

### Escalation Timing

- **Escalate early, not urgently.** An escalation sent on Monday with "we need this by Friday" is professional. An escalation sent on Thursday with "we need this by tomorrow" is a failure of anticipation.
- **Never surprise your own leadership.** Tell your partner/engagement lead about the escalation before the client sees it. Always.
- **Pair escalation with proof of effort.** "We've tried X, Y, and Z" before "we need your help with W." Nobody wants to solve a problem you haven't attempted.

### Escalation Anti-patterns

| Anti-pattern | What it signals | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Escalating without options | "I can't handle this" | Always bring at least two options, even imperfect ones |
| Escalating by CC'ing a senior person | Passive-aggressive, avoids owning the escalation | Direct email to the decision-maker with clear ask |
| Escalating everything | Inability to triage | Reserve escalation for genuine authority/resource gaps |
| Escalating nothing | Either nothing goes wrong (unlikely) or you're hiding problems | Establish a threshold: "anything that moves the timeline by >1 week or budget by >10% gets escalated" |
| Escalating to the wrong level | Burning senior attention on junior problems, or under-escalating | Match the escalation to the smallest person who can solve it |

## Steering Committee Decks

### Purpose

A steering committee exists for three reasons: decisions, visibility, and air cover. The deck serves all three.

- **Decisions:** Surface 1-3 decisions needed, with options and recommendations pre-socialized
- **Visibility:** Give sponsors enough information to defend the project in conversations you're not in
- **Air cover:** Create a documented record that risks were raised, decisions were made, and the committee was informed

### Deck Structure (8-12 slides max)

1. **Executive summary** (1 slide) — Overall status, key message, top decision needed. A senior person who reads only this slide should know the state of the project.
2. **Progress dashboard** (1-2 slides) — Milestones, RAG status, burn-down or timeline view. Visual, not text-heavy.
3. **Key accomplishments** (1 slide) — What was delivered since last SteerCo. Concrete outputs, not activities. "Completed model inventory covering 47 AI/ML systems" not "Continued inventory workstream."
4. **Risks and issues** (1-2 slides) — Named, owned, with status and mitigation. The risk register is the most-read section. Treat it accordingly.
5. **Decisions needed** (1 slide) — Each decision: context (2 sentences), options (2-3), recommendation, deadline. This is the most important slide.
6. **Next period plan** (1 slide) — What's coming, what the dependencies are, what could go wrong.
7. **Appendix** (as needed) — Detailed data, supporting analysis. Nobody reads this in the meeting. It exists for the record and for the one person who will read it afterward.

### Steering Committee Heuristics

- **Pre-wire every decision.** The steering committee meeting is for ratification, not deliberation. Have the 1:1 conversations before the meeting. If you're genuinely surprised by a reaction in SteerCo, you didn't prepare.
- **One narrative, not a collection of workstreams.** The deck tells a story: "We're here, this is why, here's where we're going, here's what we need." Workstream-by-workstream updates lose the forest. Lead with the story, then drill into workstreams if asked.
- **The risk slide is your credibility.** A risk register with no red items or no new items signals one of: nothing is actually risky (unlikely), you're hiding problems (dangerous), or you're not looking hard enough (incompetent). Show real risks. Showing that you see problems and have mitigation plans builds more trust than showing a clean register.
- **Leave time for questions.** A steering committee where the deck runs to the buzzer and there's no discussion time is a failure. Budget 40% of the meeting for discussion, not 10%.
- **Never read the slides.** The deck is a reference. You tell the story. Looking at slides and reading them aloud is the single most common consulting presentation failure.
- **Track decisions explicitly.** End the meeting with a verbal summary: "We agreed X, Y, and Z. I'll circulate the minutes by [date]." Unrecorded decisions are undecided.

## Status Report Cadence and Content

### Cadence Calibration

| Engagement phase | Recommended cadence | Why |
|---|---|---|
| First 2 weeks | Daily pulse + weekly formal | Building trust, establishing rhythm, client doesn't know you yet |
| Steady state | Weekly formal | Standard governance |
| Crisis / recovery | Daily formal | Increased visibility until stable |
| Long-running (>6 months) | Biweekly formal, weekly informal | Avoids update fatigue; trust is established |
| Wind-down / handover | Weekly formal with handover tracker | Accountability through the transition |

**The cadence negotiation:** Agree the cadence explicitly in week one. "I'll send a weekly status email on Fridays and we'll do a 30-minute check-in on Tuesdays. Does that work?" Unspoken cadence expectations lead to: client thinks you're silent, you think they're informed.

### Content Calibration by Audience

The same week, reported three ways:

| Audience | What they need | What to exclude |
|---|---|---|
| **Client project team** | Detailed tasks, dependencies, who's doing what | Internal consulting team dynamics, fee discussions |
| **Client sponsor/SteerCo** | Health, risks, decisions, value delivered | Task-level detail, technical minutiae |
| **Your partner/engagement lead** | Commercial health, client satisfaction signals, risks to the firm | Everything they need to defend the project upward |

**The information never flows down.** What you tell your partner, you may not tell the client. What you tell the client sponsor, you may not tell the project team. But information should always flow up — your partner should know everything you've told the client, plus more.

## Managing Up vs. Managing Across

### Managing Up (Partner/Engagement Lead)

Your partner's needs are different from the client's. They need to know:

- **Is the client happy?** Not "are deliverables on track" — are they actually happy? There's a gap.
- **Is the commercial position secure?** Will this engagement extend? Is there follow-on work?
- **Are there risks I need to manage?** Anything that could become a client complaint, a scope dispute, or a write-off.
- **What decisions do I need to make?** Staffing, scope, pricing, client relationship moves.

**Heuristics:**
- **No surprises.** If the partner hears about a problem from the client before they hear it from you, you've failed. Always brief your leadership before the client knows.
- **Bring solutions, not just problems.** "We have a timeline risk. Here's my mitigation plan. I need your approval to [specific action]."
- **Report client temperature, not just project metrics.** "Deliverables are on track but the client sponsor seems disengaged — I'm going to suggest a 1:1 to re-anchor."
- **Ask for coaching, not just approvals.** "I'm not sure how to handle the client's request for additional scope without a change order. How would you approach this?" This builds the relationship and often gets you better guidance than figuring it out alone.

### Managing Across (Peer Consultants, Client Counterparts)

Lateral relationships run on reciprocity and coordination, not authority.

**Heuristics:**
- **Share information generously.** The consultant who hoards context to seem indispensable becomes the bottleneck. Share what you learn, especially when it helps their workstream.
- **Coordinate before escalating.** If a peer's workstream is blocking yours, talk to them first. Escalating without a conversation first burns the lateral relationship.
- **Make them look good.** In joint presentations, credit their contributions explicitly. "As [name]'s team identified in the data quality review..." This costs nothing and buys enormous goodwill.
- **Align on the narrative before the meeting.** If you and a peer are presenting to the same SteerCo, agree on the overall story beforehand. Contradictory narratives from the same consulting team are catastrophic.
- **Respect their client relationships.** If you need something from a client stakeholder who's their primary contact, ask them to facilitate, don't go direct. Going around a peer to their client contact is a relationship violation that won't be forgotten.

## Delivering Bad News

### The Fundamental Rule

**Bad news gets worse with age.** A delay reported in week 1 is a "heads up." The same delay reported in week 4 is a "cover-up." The information hasn't changed — the trust damage has.

### The Bad News Framework

1. **Lead with the conclusion.** "The timeline has moved. Phase 2 will be delivered three weeks later than planned." Don't build up to it. The audience reads your hedging as evasion.
2. **Explain the cause in one sentence.** "The data migration took longer than estimated because the source systems had undocumented dependencies." Not a paragraph of justification.
3. **Own what's yours.** "We should have identified this risk earlier in discovery." Do not deflect blame to the client even if it's their fault — that conversation happens privately, not in the bad-news moment.
4. **Present the recovery plan.** "Here's the revised timeline. We've added [specific measure] to prevent this from recurring." The plan is what turns bad news from a crisis into a managed situation.
5. **Name what you need.** "To hold the new date, we need [specific decision/resource] by [date]." Give the client agency — they need something to do with the anxiety.

### Types of Bad News (each has a different shape)

**Budget overrun:**
- Lead with the number and the cause. "We're projecting a 15% overrun driven by the additional data quality remediation that wasn't in the original scope."
- Always connect to scope change, not inefficiency. Even if there was inefficiency, the narrative must be: "additional work was required because X." Clients accept scope growth; they don't accept poor estimation.
- Present options: absorb some, reduce scope elsewhere, or approve the overrun. Never present only "we need more money."

**Timeline slip:**
- Lead with the new date, not the delay. "Phase 2 will deliver on April 30" is better than "Phase 2 is 3 weeks late." The first is forward-looking; the second is backward-looking.
- Show what's been accomplished despite the slip. "We're delivering the full model inventory and risk classification, which required 47 systems instead of the estimated 25."
- Offer to trade scope for time if possible. "We can hold the original date if we defer the vendor assessment to Phase 3."

**Scope change:**
- Frame as a discovery, not a mistake. "Our assessment revealed that the scope needs to include X, which wasn't visible at proposal stage."
- Quantify the impact: additional time, cost, and resources.
- Present the change order as protecting the client's investment: "Including X now prevents a more costly remediation later."

**Quality issue / something went wrong:**
- Be direct and specific about what happened and what was affected.
- Separate impact from cause. The client cares about impact first, cause second.
- Present the fix, the timeline for the fix, and the preventive measure.
- Offer a concession if appropriate (fee reduction, additional work at no cost). The offer matters more than the amount.

### Bad News Anti-patterns

| Anti-pattern | What happens | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **The slow reveal** | Dripping bad news across multiple updates hoping nobody notices the trend | Rip the bandaid. One communication, full picture. |
| **The blame shift** | "The client didn't provide the data on time" (true but destructive) | Own the situation. Raise client dependencies as risks in advance, not as excuses after the fact. |
| **The buried lede** | Bad news on page 7 of a 12-page status report | Lead with it. The first thing they read should be the most important thing. |
| **The verbal-only dodge** | Delivering bad news in a meeting with no written record | Always follow up in writing. "As discussed, here is the revised timeline and the decisions we agreed." |
| **The premature solution** | Jumping to "here's the fix" before acknowledging the impact | Let the client react. Acknowledge the impact. Then present the fix. Rushing past the emotional moment makes it feel dismissed. |
| **The committee ambush** | First time the sponsor hears bad news is in the steering committee | Always deliver bad news 1:1 to the sponsor before any group forum. Always. |

## If-Then Triggers

| If... | Then... |
|---|---|
| Client hasn't responded to your last two status reports | Call, don't email a third time. Ask: "Are these updates useful? Should we adjust the format or cadence?" Silence is disengagement, not satisfaction. |
| Your engagement lead asks you to keep something off the status report | Push back once: "I'm concerned that not reporting this creates risk for us if it surfaces later." Then comply if they insist — they own the client relationship. But document your push-back. |
| A client stakeholder disagrees with your recommendation in the steering committee | Don't debate in the room. Say: "That's a fair challenge. Let me take that away and come back with analysis that addresses your concern." You win more by deferring gracefully than by winning the argument. |
| You're writing a status report and everything is genuinely on track | Still find something to flag. A future risk, a dependency, an observation. An "all green, no concerns" report reads as either not paying attention or not being candid. |
| You need to deliver bad news and the client relationship is already strained | Deliver it in person (or video, not email). Bring your engagement lead. Show more mitigation than the situation strictly requires. Invest disproportionately in the recovery plan. |
| A client-side team member is not performing and it's blocking your workstream | Never raise individual performance with the client sponsor directly. Raise the dependency gap: "We need someone with X capability available Y hours per week. Can we discuss how to resource this?" Let the client manage their own people. |
| Two client stakeholders want different things from the engagement | Surface the tension explicitly but neutrally: "I want to flag that we've received different guidance on X from [person A] and [person B]. Can we align on which direction to take?" Don't pick a side. Let the client resolve internal politics. |
| The scope is creeping and there's no change order mechanism | Raise it early and frame it protectively: "We're happy to take on the additional work — I want to make sure we document the scope change so neither side is surprised at billing time." This is protecting the client relationship, not being difficult. |

## Failure Modes

1. **The reporting machine.** Sends status reports religiously but never picks up the phone. Reports are necessary but not sufficient — the relationship lives in conversations, not documents.

2. **The over-communicator.** Sends 15 emails a day, copies everyone, creates noise that makes signal invisible. Clients learn to ignore everything. Communicate less, but make every communication count.

3. **The perfectionist.** Spends 3 hours polishing a weekly status report that the client reads in 90 seconds. Match effort to impact. A clear, concise, ugly email beats a beautiful, late deck.

4. **The diplomat.** So careful about maintaining the relationship that they never say anything uncomfortable. The client deserves honesty. Diplomatic honesty builds trust; permanent diplomacy builds a facade that collapses at the first real problem.

5. **The lone operator.** Doesn't brief their own team, doesn't coordinate narratives, sends client communications that surprise their colleagues. In consulting, uncoordinated communication is unprofessional communication.

6. **The template follower.** Uses the firm's status report template without adapting content to this client's actual needs. Templates are starting points, not destinations. If the template doesn't serve the client, change the template.

7. **The assumption maker.** Assumes the client understood the technical explanation because they nodded. Assumes the partner is aligned because they didn't object. Assumes the risk was received because it was written in the report. Always verify understanding explicitly. (See hermenia: false agreement.)

## The First Engagement (First 30 Days Communication)

Special patterns for the initial period when everything is being established.

- **Over-communicate by 50%.** More updates, more check-ins, more "just confirming." You can dial it back once trust is established. You cannot recover from a client who felt uninformed in week 2.
- **Establish the rhythm explicitly.** "Here's how I plan to keep you informed: weekly email Fridays, 30-min call Tuesdays, SteerCo monthly. Work for you?" Don't assume.
- **Ask how the previous consultant communicated.** If there was one. Learn what worked and what annoyed. You inherit communication expectations, not just deliverables.
- **Mirror the client's communication style.** If they write long emails, match the depth. If they prefer bullet points, compress. If they use WhatsApp, use WhatsApp. This is not sycophancy — it's reducing friction. (See hermenia: audience modelling.)
- **Send the first status report early.** Don't wait for "enough to report." A status report that says "Week 1: completed onboarding, met with X, Y, and Z, here's what I'm learning" establishes the cadence and signals professionalism.
